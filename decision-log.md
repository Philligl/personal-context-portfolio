# Decision Log

## How I Make Decisions

**Naturally indecisive from fear of being wrong, but actively working past it** — You're naturally indecisive because you're afraid of being wrong. This drove the paralysis by analysis that colleagues accurately called out. You've worked on this and improved significantly. You now trust your gut more because you've seen patterns—you have pattern recognition built on experience, and that reduces the fear. You're willing to talk things through, but your process is internal; you tend to turn decisions over mentally, looking from different angles, until you arrive at an answer. Then you bring the answer rather than the exploratory process, which sometimes leaves others confused not because they can't follow, but because they haven't been exposed to the information you've already absorbed.

You do sleep on significant decisions, especially early in your decision-making process.

## What I Need Before Deciding

**Balanced analysis with essential reframing** — You want to see that someone has acknowledged both pros and cons. You've worked with too many people who gave you a sales pitch for their desired decision rather than a genuine analysis. Before deciding, you like to probe whether the question needs reframing: Are we at the essence of the issue, or are we solving the wrong problem? This reframing step is often where you find the real decision.

## How I Handle Uncertainty

**Two active strategies to prevent analysis paralysis:**

**1. Bound the problem with key open questions** — When you're stuck, you force yourself to identify one or two open questions that you're willing to use as the basis for the decision. This is your protection against endless analysis: you can always ask more questions, so you explicitly bound the scope. Once you've identified those one or two key questions, you decide based on them rather than waiting for perfect information.

**2. Set a firm deadline** — When a deadline is imposed or you set one, it usually works because you already know enough. The deadline concentrates the mind and forces closure.
