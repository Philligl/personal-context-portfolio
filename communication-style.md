# Communication Style

## Overall Style

**Contextual and detailed, struggles with natural organization** — You lead with context and detail rather than stripping things down to minimum. You don't have a naturally organized mind, and it shows—both in how you think and how you communicate, verbally and in writing. You compensate by using drafts and editing heavily to beat clarity into shape. You wish you were better at this naturally, but you've learned to get there through iteration.

**Folksy by default, but calibrated** — Your natural tone is conversational and folksy. You work to moderate this depending on context, but you're aware it can go too far in either direction. You don't usually shift tone across recipients since they're fairly homogenous in reading level and comprehension; you do shift when you encounter significantly more or less knowledge than typical.

**Demanding on grammar and clarity** — You insist on good grammar. You notice passive voice and aren't bothered by it until it becomes convoluted or murky. You react strongly to writing that's unclear, disorganized, or inflammatory. If it's clear, well-organized, and not inflammatory, you're usually satisfied.

## Writing Tendencies

**Organization is intentional, not instinctive** — You value organization deeply but acknowledge it doesn't come naturally. You get there through drafting and editing, not on the first pass. Your drafts often develop as body → explanation, then you have to backtrack and clarify the "why" as an introduction.

**Mixed paragraphs and bullets, strategically used** — You combine paragraphs and bullet points. Bullets help readers immediately see whether they're absorbing 3 points or 10. This clarity of scope is important to you.

**Vocabulary matched to purpose** — You use complex vocabulary when it condenses and informs. You hate indiscriminate big words or showing off. You're sensitive to when writers are talking down or using language to hide weakness rather than clarify meaning.

**Reaction to craft matters** — You see writing as a craft and love to read good writing. You acknowledge you give up style and craft because you don't have the talent. You react strongly to writing that feels like it's talking down, showing off, or being judgmental and sanctimonious.

## Formatting Preferences

**Lead with the ask, but add structure** — You try to get to the punchline in the opening 2-3 sentences, but acknowledge organization is hard. Drafts need structure to be clear: opening statement of purpose, body, supporting explanation, conclusion.

## What I Dislike

**Fake or corporate-sounding language** — Rarely bothers you, but you'd notice if it happened frequently.

**Convoluted passive voice** — Passive voice doesn't bother you; convoluted passive voice does.

**Talking down, showing off, or sanctimoniousness** — You react strongly to writing that condescends, shows off, or is judgmental and sanctimonious.

**Disorganized or unclear structure** — This is your main frustration with others' writing.

## By Context

**Fairly consistent** — You don't usually shift tone across recipients because your audience is fairly homogenous. You do shift when you encounter significantly more or less knowledge than typical. Your baseline is folksy but moderately calibrated for a professional context.

## Signature Patterns

**No signature words or phrases** — You don't have distinctly recognizable verbal tics.

**What you avoid** — You're not a slang user, and you avoid flowery language or performative enthusiasm.

**What you seek** — Clarity, organization, good grammar, appropriate vocabulary, respect for the reader's time and intelligence.

**Meta pattern** — You're willing to work iteratively with an AI to improve your writing. You see this as a learning opportunity, not a burden.
